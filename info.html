INFORMATION

  • Dduk-guk

    Every Lunar New Year, it is one of the tradition to eat "Dduk-guk" in Korea.
    The long rice cake used for rice cake soup is said to have a meaning to increase
    your fortune by that much and to live a long life without disease, just as you pull
    a rice cake straight.

  • Song-pyun

    Song-pyeon is a traditional food eaten during Chuseok which Korean's Thansgiving day.
    When making Song-pyeon, white rice flour is used to make mochi dough. You can make
    red and yellow or any color Song-pyeon by mixing colored grains with rice flour to
    make a mochi dough. To make Song-pyeon using various grains, add 250g rice flour
    mixed with grains and stir. After that, add a little salt then add about 300ml of
    hot water and put it in the fridge for 1 hour. Stir-fry the sesame seeds and beans
    to make the stuffing, grind in a blender, and mix 50g sugar, 30g honey, and 1 tablespoon
    of water based on 100g. Take small mochi dough and add stuffing to make the Song-pyeon.
    After that, steam it in a steamer for 20 to 25 minutes, wash it in cold water once and
    apply sesame oil, and enjoy.

  • Pat-juk

    When it is the shortest day of the year in the northern hemisphere, Koreans eat Pat-juk.
    Pat-juk is made with red beans. Traditionally, in Korean folk beliefs, red is the color
    that ghosts fear, so Pat-juk (red bean porridge) boiled with red bean was considered to have
    a mysterious ability to fight bad luck. Therefore, the custom of boiling and eating Pat-juk
    had a magical meaning to prevent misfortune or contagious diseases.

    You need red beans, glutinous rice flour, water, salt, and sugar.
    1. Boil the freshly washed red beans until they are soft and cool them. When the red beans are
    cool, grind them and sift through mesh strainer.
    2. Put the red beans to the pot then pour water and boil.
    3. When eating, season with sugar or salt according to your preference.
